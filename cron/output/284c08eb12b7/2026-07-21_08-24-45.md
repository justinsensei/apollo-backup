          "text": "As for how I'm using the word \"game\" I'm using it pretty broadly and in contrast to the word \"tool.\" And it's more of a spectrum than a binary - you can have products that are pure games or pure tools, but you can also have products that blend elements of each.\n\nThe key distinction, in my mind:\n\u2022 A *tool* is tightly focused on _getting things done_. The goals of the tool are _external to_ the tool. When you swing a hammer to drive a nail, the goal is to drive the nail, and the meta-goal might be to build a birdhouse or something like that.\n\u2022 A *game* is focused on providing _entertainment_ and _emotional satisfaction_. The goals are artificial and only matter inside the rules of the game. When you swing a hammer to drive a nail in a competition to see who can drive the most nails in a minute, that's a _game_. The only reason swinging the hammer matters in that context is because you want to win (according to the rules of the game).\nWhat I'm calling \"learning games\" are always going to be a blend of these two things.\n\u2022 Duolingo is partly a *tool* because people use it to accomplish a goal that's valuable outside the rules of Duolingo the game (learning a language).\n\u2022 Duolingo is partly a *game* because it includes a bunch of artificial (but fun and satisfying!) goals that are primarily valuable because of the game's rules (rise up the leaderboard; maintain a streak; etc).\nIn this context, the most effective *game mechanics* for learning games will _reinforce_ behaviors that also contribute to the goals of the *tool*. Match-3 games can be pretty fun, but the match-3 mechanic doesn't help you learn a language, so it has no place in a language-learning game.\n\nFor most learning games, the *core loop* is where the learning actually happens. It's the least-gamified, most tool-like part of the experience - but as you'll see in apps like Duolingo, this can still be very heavily gamified! The *outer loops* are generally more game-like and less tool-like, but again there are exceptions - imagine a game mechanic where you unlock new \"core loops\" that let you practice different things in different ways (once you're pedagogically ready for that)."

          "text": "As for how I'm using the word \"game\" I'm using it pretty broadly and in contrast to the word \"tool.\" And it's more of a spectrum than a binary - you can have products that are pure games or pure tools, but you can also have products that blend elements of each.\n\nThe key distinction, in my mind:\n\u2022 A *tool* is tightly focused on _getting things done_. The goals of the tool are _external to_ the tool. When you swing a hammer to drive a nail, the goal is to drive the nail, and the meta-goal might be to build a birdhouse or something like that.\n\u2022 A *game* is focused on providing _entertainment_ and _emotional satisfaction_. The goals are artificial and only matter inside the rules of the game. When you swing a hammer to drive a nail in a competition to see who can drive the most nails in a minute, that's a _game_. The only reason swinging the hammer matters in that context is because you want to win (according to the rules of the game).\nWhat I'm calling \"learning games\" are always going to be a blend of these two things.\n\u2022 Duolingo is partly a *tool* because people use it to accomplish a goal that's valuable outside the rules of Duolingo the game (learning a language).\n\u2022 Duolingo is partly a *game* because it includes a bunch of artificial (but fun and satisfying!) goals that are primarily valuable because of the game's rules (rise up the leaderboard; maintain a streak; etc).\nIn this context, the most effective *game mechanics* for learning games will _reinforce_ behaviors that also contribute to the goals of the *tool*. Match-3 games can be pretty fun, but the match-3 mechanic doesn't help you learn a language, so it has no place in a language-learning game.\n\nFor most learning games, the *core loop* is where the learning actually happens. It's the least-gamified, most tool-like part of the experience - but as you'll see in apps like Duolingo, this can still be very heavily gamified! The *outer loops* are generally more game-like and less tool-like, but again there are exceptions - imagine a game mechanic where you unlock new \"core loops\" that let you practice different things in different ways (once you're pedagogically ready for that)."

          "text": "As for how I'm using the word \"game\" I'm using it pretty broadly and in contrast to the word \"tool.\" And it's more of a spectrum than a binary - you can have products that are pure games or pure tools, but you can also have products that blend elements of each.\n\nThe key distinction, in my mind:\n\u2022 A *tool* is tightly focused on _getting things done_. When you swing a hammer to drive a nail, the goal is to drive the nail, and the meta-goal might be to build a birdhouse or something like that.\n\u2022 A *game* is focused on providing _entertainment_ and _emotional satisfaction_. The goals are artificial and only matter inside the rules of the game. When you swing a hammer to drive a nail in a competition to see who can drive the most nails in a minute, that's a _game_.
